01

The Aggregate Demand Curve

It looks like an ordinary demand curve — but the reason it slopes downward has nothing to do with a fixed income, because at this scale, income itself is what's being determined.

The aggregate demand (AD) curve plots every combination of the overall price level and real GDP at which total spending equals total income. It slopes downward for three genuinely distinct reasons — not because "cheaper things sell more," as in ordinary microeconomics, but because of what a change in the price level does to the whole economy at once.

Three reasons AD slopes downward

The wealth effect: your cash and savings have a fixed nominal value, but their real purchasing power shrinks when prices rise. £10 buys 10 loaves of bread at £1 each, but only 5 loaves if bread doubles to £2 — you're poorer in real terms, so you spend less.

The interest rate effect: a higher price level means people need to hold more money for everyday transactions, which raises the demand for money and pushes up interest rates — and higher rates discourage investment spending.

The real exchange rate effect: higher domestic prices make imports relatively cheaper and exports relatively more expensive abroad, shrinking net exports.

Making the wealth effect concrete

£10 in your pocket, bread at £1/loaf, buys 10 loaves. Bread price doubles to £2 — the same £10 now buys only 5 loaves. Your nominal wealth (£10) never changed; your real wealth (loaves you can afford) was cut in half. That's the entire mechanism — scaled up across every price in the economy at once.

A rise in the price level from P1 to P2 moves the economy from Point A to Point B — real income/output falls from Y1 to Y2, purely because of these three effects, holding the money supply fixed.

Check yourself
Which of the following explains why aggregate demand slopes downward due to the interest rate effect?
Correct — a higher price level means more money is needed for the same transactions, and with a fixed money supply, that extra demand for money pushes interest rates up, cooling investment spending.

GDP = C + I + G + (X−M) from Part 1 is exactly what's moving here — all three effects work by shrinking one or more of C, I, or (X−M) as the price level rises. Nothing new to memorize; the same formula, viewed dynamically.

02

The Aggregate Supply Curve

Three time horizons, three different shapes — because wages and contracts don't adjust to price changes instantly.

Three aggregate supply curves, by time horizon

Very short run (VSRAS) — flat: over a few months, firms adjust output without changing prices at all — run the factory harder or softer, keeping the price tag the same.

Short run (SRAS) — upward sloping: wages and input costs are fixed by contracts for a while, so as output prices rise, profit margins widen and firms expand production.

Long run (LRAS) — perfectly vertical: eventually wages and input costs catch up fully with prices, restoring margins to normal. Output settles back at the economy's potential GDP — determined only by available capital, labor, and technology — regardless of the price level.

The same economy, three lenses: flat in the very short run, upward-sloping in the short run, vertical in the long run. The vertical LRAS position is the economy's full-employment (potential) output — the natural rate of unemployment, not zero unemployment, but a stable, healthy churn of job seekers and openings.

What determines potential output
Y = F(K̄, L̄)

Fixed capital stock K̄ and available labor L̄, combined through the technology embedded in that capital — nothing about the price level appears in this equation at all, which is exactly why LRAS is vertical.

Check yourself
Why is the long-run aggregate supply curve vertical?
Correct — once wages and other costs catch up fully with the price level, a higher price level no longer changes real profit margins, so output settles at potential GDP regardless of price.

03

Shifts in Aggregate Demand & Aggregate Supply

A movement along a curve is a price effect. A shift in the whole curve is something new entering the picture.

What shifts AD

Anything that changes C, I, G, or (X−M) at every price level: consumer confidence, tax policy, government spending decisions, foreign income levels, exchange rate moves, or central bank interest-rate policy. A rightward shift means more is demanded at every price; a leftward shift means less.

What shifts AS

Changes in input costs (wages, energy, raw materials), productivity, or the size of the labor force. A leftward shift — say, from an oil price shock — means less is supplied at every price level; a rightward shift, from a productivity boom, means more.

Distinguishing a shift from a movement

If the price level itself changes and quantity responds along the existing curve, that's a movement along the curve. If quantity changes at every given price level — because something in the underlying economy genuinely changed — that's a shift of the whole curve. This is the identical distinction from the demand-curve module: a price change moves you along the curve; anything else shifts the whole curve.

Check yourself
A central bank cuts interest rates, encouraging more business investment at every price level. This is best described as:
Correct — lower interest rates raise investment spending (I) at every price level, shifting the whole AD curve to the right, not just moving along it.

This AD/AS framework is the direct macro-scale sibling of the demand/supply model from Economics Module 1 — same logic of movements vs. shifts, just applied to an entire nation's output and price level instead of one product's market.

04

Four Types of Macroeconomic Equilibrium

Where AD meets SRAS can land above, below, or exactly at potential GDP — and each case has its own name, causes, and investment implications.

1. Long-run full employment

AD intersects SRAS exactly on the vertical LRAS line. Output equals potential GDP; both labor and capital are fully utilized at the economy's natural, sustainable pace.

2. Recessionary gap

AD falls (shifts left) — perhaps from weaker consumer confidence — pushing equilibrium output below potential GDP. Both output and prices fall; unemployment rises. A recession is formally defined as at least two consecutive quarters of falling real GDP.

3. Inflationary gap

AD rises (shifts right) beyond what the economy can sustainably produce, pushing equilibrium output above potential GDP. Both output and prices rise; the labor market runs unusually hot.

4. Stagflation

Unlike the other three, this comes from an AS shift, not an AD shift. A leftward shift in SRAS — a sudden oil price spike, say — simultaneously drops output and raises prices, producing the uniquely painful combination of high unemployment and high inflation together.

Real-world example — the 1970s oil shocks

Oil prices quadrupled in 1973, triggering a global recession that lasted into 1975 — but unlike a typical demand-driven downturn, prices rose throughout rather than falling. Oil prices doubled again in 1979–1980, causing a second stagflationary recession in 1980–1982. Both episodes are textbook cases of an AS-driven shock: the SRAS curve shifted left, output fell, and prices rose, all at once.

An economy experiences a sudden spike in global oil prices, causing both a decline in output and a rise in the price level simultaneously. This is:
Correct — falling output combined with rising prices at the same time is the signature of an AS-driven shock (stagflation), not an AD-driven recession or boom.

05

The Production Function & Growth Accounting

Zoom out from quarters to decades, and a completely different question takes over: what actually makes an economy bigger over time?

The production function
Y = A × F(L, K)

Y = output, L = labor, K = capital stock, A = total factor productivity (TFP) — the part of growth that isn't explained by simply adding more labor or capital, largely reflecting technological progress.

Two governing assumptions

Constant returns to scale: double every input, and output exactly doubles.

Diminishing marginal productivity: holding other inputs fixed, each additional unit of any one input adds progressively less output. Pile ever more capital onto a fixed workforce, and each new machine contributes less than the last.

Why this matters for growth policy: diminishing returns mean an economy can never sustain long-run growth just by piling up capital relative to labor — that strategy runs into steadily worse returns. The only way to sustain per-capita growth indefinitely is genuine TFP growth: technological progress that shifts the entire production function upward.

The growth accounting equation
Growth in potential GDP = Growth in TFP + WL(Growth in labor) + WK(Growth in capital)

WL and WK are labor's and capital's shares of national income — for many developed economies, roughly 0.7 and 0.3 respectively.

Worked example

TFP growth 1.0%, labor grows 1.5%, capital grows 3.0%, with WL=0.7 and WK=0.3:

Growth in potential GDP = 1.0% + 0.7(1.5%) + 0.3(3.0%) = 1.0% + 1.05% + 0.90% = 2.95%. Notice labor's larger income share means the identical percentage growth rate in labor contributes more to overall growth than the same percentage growth rate in capital.

Check yourself
Why can't an economy sustain long-run per-capita growth purely by continuously adding more capital per worker?
Correct — diminishing returns to capital mean that simply deepening the capital stock relative to labor runs into steadily worse payoffs; only genuine technological progress (TFP growth) can sustain growth indefinitely.

06

Measures of Sustainable Growth

One ratio explains most of the difference in living standards between rich and poor countries.

Labor productivity
Labor productivity = Real GDP / Aggregate hours worked

Dividing the production function through by labor (L) reveals what actually drives output per worker: Y/L = A × F(1, K/L) — labor productivity depends on the capital-to-labor ratio and total factor productivity, and nothing else. Everything else in this module ultimately funnels through this one number.

Level vs. growth rate — a crucial distinction

The level of labor productivity is much higher in developed countries, reflecting decades of accumulated capital and technology — it's a stock, built up over a long history.

The growth rate of labor productivity, by contrast, is often faster in developing countries, precisely because they're starting from a lower capital base where diminishing returns haven't bitten yet — the same logic behind the "convergence" idea from the growth-accounting section.

Why analysts watch this number closely

Rising labor productivity means the same workforce can produce more, which lets companies raise wages without necessarily squeezing profit margins — a genuinely healthy combination for equity markets. Slowing productivity growth, by contrast, often forces a harder trade-off between wage growth and profitability.

Check yourself
Country A has a much higher level of labor productivity than Country B, but Country B's productivity is growing faster. What does this pattern most likely reflect?
Correct — this is the classic convergence pattern: developed economies have a higher accumulated productivity level, while developing economies, starting from less capital per worker, often grow faster as they build up their capital base.
