CFA Program · Prerequisite Economics · Module 4

The economy breathes in a rhythm — and every sector breathes differently.

Module 3 built the tools for measuring a whole economy at a single moment. This module asks how that economy moves through time — recovery, expansion, slowdown, contraction — and why consumer durables, housing, and exports each dance to a different beat within that same cycle. It closes with the two numbers every financial news report leans on constantly: the unemployment rate and the inflation rate.

01

Consumer Behavior Across the Business Cycle

Households are the largest sector of most economies — and not every kind of spending reacts to the cycle the same way.

Consumer spending splits into three categories that behave very differently as the economy moves through recovery, expansion, slowdown, and contraction:

Three spending categories, three cyclical personalities

Durables (autos, appliances, furniture): the most cyclical. Big-ticket items with a long useful life are easy to postpone when things look shaky — a weakness here is often an early warning sign.

Non-durables (food, medicine, household products): the least cyclical — people keep buying necessities regardless of the economic mood.

Services (dining out, entertainment, personal care): somewhere in between, since services mix discretionary and non-discretionary spending together.

Permanent income vs. temporary income

Permanent income excludes one-off windfalls and losses, capturing the income households believe they can reliably count on. Services spending tracks permanent income closely — people adjust their gym membership or streaming subscriptions based on their durable sense of financial security, not a one-time bonus. Durable goods spending, by contrast, tracks disposable income more directly, regardless of its source.

Reading the saving rate: a rising saving rate can mean caution (households pulling back, a bearish signal) — but a large accumulated stock of savings also builds the capacity for a future spending recovery. Watch the direction of change as much as the level.

Check yourself
Why do durable goods purchases show the most pronounced cyclical swings?
Correct — because durables can be kept in service longer with repairs, households have real flexibility to delay buying a replacement when the outlook looks uncertain.

Why analysts watch durables specifically: because they're the most sensitive spending category, a shift in durable goods orders is often one of the earliest visible signals of where the broader cycle is heading, well before broader GDP data confirms it.

02

Housing & Business Investment Behavior

Small relative to the whole economy, but volatile enough to matter far more than its size suggests.

Housing's two drivers

Interest rate sensitivity: since most homes are financed with a mortgage, housing demand is unusually sensitive to borrowing costs — lower rates directly expand affordability and pull buyers in.

Demographics: the long-run trend underneath the cycle, driven by household formation, concentrated in the 25–40 age bracket. A country with fast urbanization or a large cohort entering that age range has a demographic tailwind that can override short-term cyclical weakness.

How housing cycles can overshoot

If prices have risen briskly, buyers sometimes purchase specifically to capture expected future gains — extending the boom upward and inviting overbuilding. The resulting inventory of unsold homes eventually reverses the price trend, catching late buyers who stretched their finances thin. This exact pattern played out across many countries in the 2008–2009 financial crisis.

Business investment — the most volatile sector of all

Among consumer goods, business equipment, and household-products companies, business investment fluctuates the most with the cycle — more than either consumer spending or exports. Firms sharply expand capital spending in good times and cut it just as sharply when demand looks uncertain, since capital projects are the easiest kind of spending to delay entirely.

Check yourself
Housing demand is unusually sensitive to which factor, more than most other sectors?
Correct — because most home purchases are mortgage-financed, changes in borrowing costs directly and immediately affect affordability and demand.

Rank order of cyclical sensitivity, roughly: business investment > durable goods > housing > services > non-durables — though housing's sensitivity to interest rates specifically can make it move independently of the broader cycle at times.

03

External Trade Sector Behavior

Imports answer to the domestic cycle. Exports answer to everyone else's.

Two different masters

Imports rise and fall with domestic GDP growth — stronger domestic demand pulls in more goods from abroad.

Exports depend on economic conditions in trading partner countries, largely independent of what's happening domestically. A country's exports can grow even during a domestic slowdown, if its major trading partners are booming.

The exchange rate's independent effect
Currency appreciates → imports cheaper, exports more expensive abroad
Currency depreciates → imports pricier, exports cheaper abroad

Short-term currency wiggles barely matter for trade flows — it's sustained, multi-year currency trends that actually move the trade balance meaningfully.

Worked example — where does trade sensitivity concentrate?

A tiny, highly open economy like Singapore or Lithuania can see total trade (exports + imports) exceed its entire GDP — external cycles matter enormously there. A large, relatively closed economy like the United States or Brazil sees domestic conditions dominate far more, with trade a smaller share of the overall picture.

Check yourself
A country's major trading partners are experiencing strong economic growth, even though its own domestic economy is slowing. What is the most likely effect on this country's exports?
Correct — exports respond primarily to the economic conditions of trading partners, so strong foreign growth can keep exports rising even as the domestic economy weakens.

Why this matters for a portfolio: a company whose sales are mostly domestic tracks the local cycle closely; a genuinely global exporter can partially decouple from it — worth remembering when a stock's earnings don't match the headlines about the local economy.

04

Types & Measures of Unemployment

"Unemployed" sounds simple. The official statistic hides several genuinely different situations inside one number.

The building blocks

Employed: has a job (excludes informal-sector work).

Labor force: employed + actively job-seeking. Excludes retirees, children, students, and stay-at-home parents.

Unemployed: actively seeking work but currently jobless.

The two headline ratios
Unemployment rate = Unemployed / Labor force
Participation rate = Labor force / Working-age population
The hidden categories

Frictionally unemployed: between jobs, short-term and largely harmless — a healthy sign of a dynamic labor market, not weakness.

Long-term unemployed: jobless for an extended period (often 3–4+ months), a genuinely worse signal.

Discouraged workers: stopped looking entirely — statistically outside the labor force, and therefore invisible to the official unemployment rate, even though they represent real joblessness.

Underemployed: has a job, but overqualified or working part-time while wanting full-time — also invisible to the standard unemployment rate.

The trap: during a prolonged downturn, the unemployment rate can actually fall — not because jobs improved, but because discouraged workers stop counting as part of the labor force at all. Always read the unemployment rate alongside the participation rate to tell these two very different stories apart.

Check yourself
A lawyer who lost her corporate job takes a part-time position at a bookstore while continuing to search for legal work. How does she count in the official unemployment rate?
Correct — having any job counts as employed for the unemployment rate, regardless of whether it matches her qualifications. Underemployment is a real phenomenon, but it isn't captured by the standard unemployment statistic.

05

Why Unemployment Lags the Cycle

By the time the unemployment rate confirms a recession, the recession is usually already well underway.

Two reasons the unemployment rate lags

The labor force itself is elastic: discouraged workers drop out during downturns (flattering the rate) and flood back in during recoveries — sometimes so quickly that the unemployment rate briefly rises even as the labor market genuinely improves, purely because more people resume actively looking.

Businesses are reluctant to change headcount: firms hold onto workers during a downturn (avoiding the cost of losing trained staff) and wait to hire during a recovery until existing staff are fully utilized again — a delay in both directions.

Better leading indicators of the labor market

Overtime hours and temp staffing: businesses cut overtime and temp workers first, before touching full-time headcount — watch these for the earliest warning.

Labor productivity (output/hours): when businesses hold onto staff even as output falls, measured productivity drops — often the very first visible sign of trouble, even before hours or payrolls move.

Reading the sequence

On the way down: productivity falls first → overtime and temp staffing get cut → full-time layoffs happen last. On the way up: productivity rises first (existing staff used more efficiently) → overtime and temp hiring pick up → full-time rehiring happens last. The unemployment rate is the caboose of this whole train, not the engine.

Check yourself
During an economic recovery, the unemployment rate briefly ticks up even though job openings are genuinely increasing. What is the most likely explanation?
Correct — as conditions improve, previously discouraged workers resume actively job-seeking, and since they aren't immediately hired, they can temporarily push the unemployment rate up even during a genuine recovery.

This is exactly why unemployment is called a lagging indicator — useful for confirming where the cycle has been, far less useful for predicting where it's headed next.

06

Inflation, Deflation, Hyperinflation & Disinflation

Four related words that get mixed up constantly — and mixing them up leads to genuinely different conclusions.

Four terms, precisely defined

Inflation: a sustained rise in the overall price level.

Deflation: a sustained decrease in the price level — a negative inflation rate.

Disinflation: inflation is still positive, just slowing down — say, from 18% to 6%. Prices are still rising, only more gently. This is not the same thing as deflation at all.

Hyperinflation: an extreme, runaway version of inflation — commonly 500% to 1,000%+ per year.

Why deflation is genuinely dangerous

Debt contracts are fixed in nominal terms — so when prices fall, the real burden of existing debt actually rises. Cash-strapped, over-indebted firms respond by slashing spending, investment, and jobs, which deepens the very contraction causing the deflation in the first place — a vicious spiral. This is exactly why most developed-economy central banks target roughly 2% inflation rather than 0%: it keeps a buffer against ever tipping into deflation.

What actually causes hyperinflation

Almost always the same root cause: large government spending not backed by real tax revenue, financed instead by printing money. As prices spiral, people rush to convert cash into real goods before it loses more value, which only accelerates the money's velocity and the price spiral further. The only real fix is regaining control of the money supply.

Check yourself
A country's inflation rate falls from 22% to 9% over two years. Prices are still rising, just more slowly. This is:
Correct — the inflation rate is still positive (prices still rising), just at a slower pace — the definition of disinflation, distinct from deflation.

Deflation has happened for real: the US during the Great Depression, briefly after the 2008–2009 financial crisis, and Japan repeatedly since the late 1990s — none of these were minor or theoretical events.

07

Measuring Inflation: The Construction of Price Indexes

A price index is just the value of a fixed shopping basket, tracked over time.

Worked example — a two-good basket

January 2019: 50 kg rice at ¥3/kg, 70 liters gasoline at ¥4.4/liter. Basket value = (50×3) + (70×4.4) = ¥458.

February 2019, same quantities: 50 kg rice at ¥4/kg, 70 liters gasoline at ¥4.5/liter. Basket value = (50×4) + (70×4.5) = ¥515.

Setting January's index to 100: February's index = (515/458) × 100 = 112.45. Inflation rate = (112.45/100) − 1 = 12.45%.

Three biases in a fixed-basket (Laspeyres) index

Substitution bias: when a good's price rises, people shift toward cheaper substitutes — a fixed basket misses this, overstating true inflation.

Quality bias: if a product's quality improves over time (safer, more reliable cars) without adjustment, rising prices get mistaken entirely for inflation rather than partly reflecting genuine improvement.

New product bias: a fixed basket, set years ago, simply can't include products that didn't exist yet.

All three biases push in the same direction: a fixed-basket index tends to overstate true inflation. Statistical agencies partially correct for this with hedonic pricing (adjusting for quality) and chained index formulas (adjusting for substitution).

Check yourself
Beef prices rise sharply, and consumers shift much of their spending toward chicken instead. A fixed-basket price index that doesn't account for this shift will tend to:
Correct — this is substitution bias: the fixed basket keeps weighting the now-more-expensive beef as heavily as before, missing the cost savings consumers actually achieve by switching to chicken.

08

Cost-Push vs. Demand-Pull Inflation

Two completely different engines can drive the same rising price level — and they call for very different responses.

Cost-push inflation

Rising input costs — especially wages growing faster than productivity — squeeze profit margins, and businesses raise prices to protect them. This is precisely the leftward AS-shift mechanism behind stagflation from Module 3, Part 2.

Unit labor cost (ULC)
ULC = W / O

W = total labor compensation per hour, O = output per hour (productivity). If wage growth outpaces productivity growth, ULC rises — a direct, trackable cost-push inflation signal.

Demand-pull inflation

Aggregate demand grows faster than the economy's capacity to supply it, straining resources and creating bottlenecks. The closer actual GDP sits to potential GDP (the higher the capacity utilization), the more likely excess demand shows up as rising prices instead of rising output.

The Monetarist view

"Inflation results when too much money chases too few goods." A surplus of money in circulation lowers money's own value, the same way a surplus of anything else lowers its price — and since money's value is expressed in terms of what it buys, a falling money value shows up as a rising overall price level.

Check yourself
Wages across an economy grow 6% while productivity grows only 2%. What does this combination most directly signal?
Correct — when compensation growth outpaces productivity growth, unit labor costs rise, squeezing margins and creating exactly the cost-push pressure that tends to show up as higher prices.

Leading, Coincident & Lagging Economic Indicators

You already learned unemployment lags the cycle. Here's the full, formal classification it belongs to — and the specific data series analysts actually watch.

Three timing categories

Leading indicators turn before the overall economy does — their value is in forecasting where the cycle is headed next.

Coincident indicators turn at roughly the same time as the overall economy — useful for confirming where the economy actually stands right now.

Lagging indicators turn after the overall economy already has — useful for confirming a trend once it's already well underway, not for predicting it.

No indicator is perfect. Every one of these series gets revised periodically after its initial release, and turning points are only ever confirmed well after the fact — even a "leading" indicator can send a false signal. Analysts typically watch a basket of several at once rather than trusting any single series in isolation.

Why the yield curve counts as a leading indicator

The spread between long-term and short-term bond yields is one of the most closely watched leading indicators precisely because it reflects investors' collective forward-looking expectations — when short-term rates rise above long-term rates (an inverted curve), markets are often pricing in a future slowdown well before it shows up in actual GDP or employment data.

Check yourself
Industrial production and non-agricultural employment are examples of which type of economic indicator?
Correct — these series move in step with the broader economy, making them useful for confirming its current state rather than predicting or lagging it.
Nowcasting

Official GDP figures are only published well after the period they describe. Nowcasting combines real-time indicators — the ones above, plus higher-frequency data — to produce a continuously updated estimate of the economy's present state, without waiting for the official, lagged data release.

Theories of the Business Cycle

Everyone agrees the economy cycles. Economists genuinely disagree about why — and what, if anything, a government should do about it.

Module 3 built the AD/AS framework. These three schools of thought are really three different answers to the same question: when the economy dips, which curve moved, and should anyone try to move it back?

Neoclassical & Real Business Cycle (RBC) theory — a supply-side story

Fluctuations come from shifts in aggregate supply — an input price spike shifting AS left, or new technology shifting it right. Left alone, the economy gradually converges back to its new equilibrium on its own. Government intervention is generally seen as unnecessary, and possibly counterproductive — it can exacerbate the very fluctuation it's trying to fix, or delay the natural convergence.

Keynesian theory — a demand-side story

Fluctuations come from shifts in aggregate demand — a collapse in consumer or business confidence shifting AD left. Left alone, a weak economy can get stuck well below potential GDP for a long time. Keynesians advocate active government intervention — fiscal or monetary stimulus — specifically to restore full employment and head off a deflationary spiral.

Monetarist theory — trust the money supply, not the timing

Monetarists share some common ground with the Neoclassical view: the timing and impact of discretionary government intervention is highly uncertain, and poorly-timed intervention can do more harm than good. Rather than actively steering AD or AS, Monetarists argue it's generally better to let the economy find its own equilibrium unassisted, while keeping the money supply growing at a steady, predictable pace.

Same recession, three different prescriptions

A demand-driven downturn hits. A Neoclassical/RBC economist expects the economy to self-correct and generally advises against intervention. A Keynesian economist sees a stuck, underperforming economy and advocates for active fiscal or monetary stimulus to close the gap. A Monetarist agrees intervention timing is genuinely hard to get right, and prefers a steady, rules-based approach to money growth over discretionary fine-tuning.
