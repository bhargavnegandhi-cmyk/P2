Every dollar spent is a dollar earned by someone else.

GDP sounds like an abstract statistic, but it's built from the same circular flow of money you already understand: someone spends, someone else receives it as income, and that income eventually gets spent again. Part 1 builds GDP from the ground up — how to measure it three equivalent ways, how to strip out the effect of inflation, and the accounting identity linking a nation's saving, investment, government budget, and trade balance into one equation.

01

What Is GDP?

Three different questions, one identical answer.

Gross domestic product measures the market value of all final goods and services produced within an economy during a period. That's the output definition. But every dollar spent buying that output becomes income for someone — a wage, a rent payment, interest, or profit. So GDP is equally the income definition: the total income earned by everyone in the economy. And since every dollar of that income traces back to someone spending it, GDP is also total expenditure. Three names, one number.

A simplified economy: households supply labor and capital to firms in exchange for £100 of income; firms use those inputs to produce £100 of goods and services, which households buy right back. Output, income, and expenditure are all exactly £100 — the same money, counted three different ways as it circulates.

Three rules that keep GDP consistent

Only current production counts. A used car sale, a stock's capital gain, and government transfer payments (unemployment benefits) are all excluded — nothing new was produced.

Only market transactions count. Unpaid housework, gardening, or your own home cooking never appear in GDP, however valuable they are, because there's no market price to record.

Only final goods count — more on this in the next section, since it's the trickiest rule to apply correctly.

Check yourself
A government sends a $1,000 unemployment benefit check to a laid-off worker. Does this transaction get counted in GDP?
Correct — transfer payments move money without any corresponding new production, so they're excluded from GDP entirely.

Why this matters for an analyst: whichever of the three lenses you use — output, income, or expenditure — you're describing the identical economic activity. Government statistical agencies actually compute GDP through both the income and expenditure approaches independently, as a built-in consistency check.

02

The Value-Added Method

A loaf of bread changes hands four times before it reaches you. GDP only wants to count it once.

A final good is sold to its ultimate user. An intermediate good gets resold or used to make something else. If GDP counted every sale at every stage, the same underlying value would be counted again and again — this is exactly the double-counting problem the "final goods only" rule is designed to prevent.

Worked example — from wheat to bread
StageReceipts (€)Value added (€)
Farmer sells wheat to miller0.150.15
Miller sells flour to baker0.460.31
Baker sells bread to retailer0.780.32
Retailer sells bread to customer1.000.22
Total—1.00

GDP counts the €1.00 final sale price exactly once — or, equivalently, sums each stage's value added (0.15+0.31+0.32+0.22), which lands on the identical €1.00. The wheat and flour never separately appear in GDP; their value is already baked into the final loaf's price.

Worked example — an automobile

Materials cost $5,000 (steel, plastics, semiconductors combined), assembly brings the manufacturer's price to $15,000, wholesale to $16,000, and retail to $18,000. Summing raw sales at every stage gives $54,000 — but that wildly overstates GDP's actual contribution.

The correct answer is simply the final retail price, $18,000 — equivalently, the sum of value added at each step: $5,000 (materials) + $10,000 (assembly) + $1,000 (wholesale markup) + $2,000 (retail markup) = $18,000.

Check yourself
A lumber mill sells $200 of wood to a furniture maker, who builds a chair and sells it to a retailer for $500, who then sells it to a customer for $800. What is this chair's contribution to GDP?
Correct — only the final sale to the end customer, $800, counts toward GDP. Summing the value added at each stage (200+300+300=800) gives the identical answer.

03

Nominal vs. Real GDP & the GDP Deflator

If prices rise 7% and output doesn't change at all, did the economy actually grow? GDP alone can't tell you — you need to strip out inflation first.

Nominal vs. real GDP
Nominal GDPt = Pt × Qt
Real GDPt = Pbase × Qt

Nominal GDP uses this year's prices. Real GDP uses base-year prices held fixed — so real GDP only moves when actual output moves.

Worked example — an economy that only makes cars

2019: 300,000 cars at €18,750 each. 2020: still 300,000 cars, but the average price rises 7% to €20,062.50.

Nominal GDP2019 = 300,000 × €18,750 = €5,625,000,000. Nominal GDP2020 = 300,000 × €20,062.50 = €6,018,750,000 — an apparent 7% "growth."

But Real GDP2020 (valued at 2019 prices) = 300,000 × €18,750 = €5,625,000,000 — unchanged. The economy didn't grow at all; only prices did.

The GDP deflator
GDP deflator = (Nominal GDP / Real GDP) × 100

A deflator of 107 means the overall price level is 7% above the base year. It's one of the broadest available gauges of economy-wide inflation.

Now add real growth on top

If 2020 production actually rises 3% (to 309,000 cars) on top of the 7% price increase: Nominal GDP2020 = 309,000 × €20,062.50 = €6,199,312,500. Real GDP2020 = 309,000 × €18,750 = €5,793,750,000 (a genuine 3% increase). GDP deflator = (6,199,312,500 / 5,793,750,000) × 100 = 107 — correctly isolating the 7% inflation from the 3% real growth, even though nominal GDP rose by a blended 10.2%.

Check yourself
Nominal GDP grew 9% this year, while the GDP deflator rose from 100 to 104. Approximately what was real GDP growth?
Correct — real GDP growth ≈ nominal growth − inflation ≈ 9% − 4% = 5% (the exact figure is (1.09/1.04)−1 ≈ 4.8%).

04

The Components of GDP

Four sectors, one formula — this is the single most-used equation in macroeconomics.

The expenditure approach to GDP
GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
The four components

C — Consumption: household spending on final goods and services. Typically the largest single component, often over half of GDP.

I — Investment: business spending on capital goods (factories, equipment) plus changes in inventory. Driven mainly by interest rates and expected profitability.

G — Government spending: government purchases of final goods and services (not transfer payments, which were already excluded in Module 01).

X − M — Net exports: exports minus imports — the country's trade balance.

The consumption function
C = C(Y − T)

Consumption depends primarily on disposable income (GDP minus net taxes). The marginal propensity to consume (MPC) is the fraction of one extra dollar of income that gets spent; the rest is saved, so MPC + MPS = 1.

Reading real-world consumption data

Household consumption as a share of GDP: United States 68.0%, Mexico 64.8%, Italy 60.3%, Canada 57.9%, Japan 55.6%, France 53.9%, Germany 52.1%. These ratios approximate each country's average propensity to consume — the higher the number, the more sensitive that economy is to changes in household income, and the larger the effect of a tax cut on total spending.

Check yourself
A household's marginal propensity to consume is 0.75. If it receives an extra $2,000 in income, how much does it save?
Correct — MPS = 1 − MPC = 1 − 0.75 = 0.25. Saving = 0.25 × $2,000 = $500.

05

Saving, Investment, the Fiscal Balance & the Trade Balance

One accounting identity ties a nation's private savings, government budget, and trade balance into a single unbreakable equation.

Since total expenditure must equal total income, a bit of algebra on GDP = C + I + G + (X−M) produces a genuinely powerful identity:

The fundamental macro identity
S = I + (G − T) + (X − M)

S = total private saving, T = net taxes. Rearranged: G − T = (S − I) − (X − M).

This says domestic private saving gets absorbed exactly three ways: funding business investment (I), financing a government deficit (G−T), or building up net claims on foreign economies via a trade surplus. If a country runs a trade deficit instead, it's effectively importing savings from abroad to make the identity balance.

Reading the rearranged identity

A government running a fiscal deficit [(G−T)>0] has exactly two possible offsetting sources, or some mix of both: the private sector saves more than it invests [(S−I)>0], or the country runs a trade deficit [(X−M)<0] and effectively borrows the difference from abroad. There's no fourth option — the identity always has to balance.

Check yourself
A country's private saving is 22% of GDP and investment is 18% of GDP, with a balanced government budget (G=T). What must be true of its trade balance?
Correct — S = I + (G−T) + (X−M) → 22 = 18 + 0 + (X−M) → (X−M) = 4% of GDP, a trade surplus, since excess private saving beyond investment must show up as net foreign lending.
