CFA Program · Prerequisite Economics · Module 5

Two levers, one goal: an economy that grows steadily without overheating.

Governments influence an entire economy through exactly two channels — a central bank managing money and credit, and a government managing taxation and spending. This module builds both from first principles: what money actually is, how a bank literally creates it out of a single deposit, and why the interest rate you see quoted on the news is really just the price of money itself.

01

Monetary Policy vs. Fiscal Policy

Two different institutions, two different tools, one shared goal.

Two policy types, two different actors

Monetary policy: central bank activities directed at influencing the quantity of money and credit in an economy.

Fiscal policy: government decisions about taxation and spending.

Both exist to steer the same overarching goal: stable, positive growth with low, stable inflation — avoiding both damaging boom-bust cycles and the human cost of high unemployment. Fiscal policy carries an extra role monetary policy doesn't: it can be used deliberately to redistribute income and wealth across society, not just to manage the business cycle.

Why governments, specifically, move markets

A single household's spending decision is invisible at the scale of a national economy. A government's isn't — for two structural reasons. First, the public sector typically employs a significant share of the population and accounts for a significant share of total spending. Second, governments are the largest borrowers in world debt markets, meaning their financing decisions directly move interest rates that everyone else has to borrow against.

Check yourself
A central bank raises its policy interest rate to cool an overheating economy. Is this monetary or fiscal policy?
Correct — actions by a central bank aimed at influencing money and credit conditions are monetary policy, distinct from a government's own taxation and spending decisions.

02

Functions & Definitions of Money

Money didn't need to be invented by decree — it emerged because barter is genuinely painful to live with.

The problem money solves — a barter economy

Without money, every trade requires a "double coincidence of wants" — you need to find someone who has what you want and wants exactly what you have. Goods may be indivisible (you can't sell "half a cow" for a loaf of bread), and there's no common measure of value: is a chicken worth three loaves of bread, or five? Every possible pair of goods needs its own separately negotiated exchange rate.

Three functions of money

Medium of exchange: a farmer can sell wheat for money, then exchange that money for wine — no need to find a wine producer who happens to want wheat specifically.

Store of wealth: money can be held and spent later, provided people continue to trust its value.

Unit of account: a single, universal measure of value — every good's price can be stated in the same units, instead of needing a separate exchange rate for every possible pair of goods.

Why gold worked so well as early money

To function as money, an asset needs to be readily acceptable, have a known value, be easily divisible, hold high value relative to its weight, and be difficult to counterfeit. Gold and silver checked every box — durable, splittable into coins, valuable in small quantities, and hard to fake — which is exactly why precious metals dominated as money for most of human history.

Narrow money vs. broad money

Narrow money: physical notes and coins in circulation, plus other very highly liquid deposits.

Broad money: narrow money plus the entire wider range of liquid assets that can realistically be used to make purchases — savings accounts, money market funds, and similar.

Check yourself
A collector holds gold coins for years, confident they'll still be valuable when sold. This best illustrates which function of money?
Correct — holding an asset over time specifically because it's expected to retain value is the store-of-wealth function.

Credit cards aren't quite money: unlike a debit card or a cheque, a credit card payment is a deferred promise to pay — a genuinely different mechanism from spending money you already hold, even though it feels identical at checkout.

03

The Money Creation Process

A single €100 deposit can turn into €1,000 of money in the economy — without a single new euro being printed.

Fractional reserve banking

Banks keep only a fraction of deposits on hand as reserves and lend out the rest — a reasonable bet, since not every depositor withdraws their money at the same time. The share kept back is the reserve requirement.

Worked example — the cascade across three banks

A customer deposits €100 at First Bank of Nations. With a 10% reserve requirement, the bank keeps €10 and lends €90. The borrower spends that €90, and the recipient deposits it at Second Bank of Nations — which keeps €9 and lends €81. That €81 gets spent, deposited at Third Bank of Nations, which keeps €8.10 and lends €72.90 — and the cascade continues.

€100 / 0.10 = €1,000 total. The multiplier itself, 1/reserve requirement, is called the money multiplier — here, 1/0.10 = 10. A smaller reserve requirement means a larger multiplier, and more money created from the same original deposit.

Check yourself
If the reserve requirement is 20% instead of 10%, how much total money is ultimately created from an initial €100 deposit?
Correct — Total money = €100/0.20 = €500. A higher reserve requirement means a smaller money multiplier (1/0.20=5) and less money created.

04

The Quantity Theory of Money

One equation ties the entire money supply to the entire economy's spending — and it isn't even a theory, technically. It's an accounting identity.

The quantity equation of exchange
M × V = P × Y

M = quantity of money, V = velocity of circulation (how many times a unit of currency changes hands in a given period), P = average price level, Y = real output.

Both sides describe the identical flow of spending, just from two different angles: the left side is "how much money changed hands, and how often," the right side is "the dollar value of everything actually produced." They must be equal by construction — this isn't a claim that could turn out false, it's an accounting identity.

Where the "theory" part comes in

The quantity theory of money adds an assumption on top of the identity: that velocity (V) is roughly constant. If that holds, spending (P×Y) moves proportionally with the money supply (M) — and if real output (Y) is also fixed in the short run, any increase in M shows up almost entirely as a rise in the price level, P. Economists who believe money growth directly drives inflation this way are called Monetarists.

The causality question isn't fully settled. It's also possible causality runs the other way: economic activity levels could determine how much money circulates, rather than the money supply driving activity. This ambiguity is exactly why some governments' attempts to control inflation purely by targeting money growth haven't always worked as cleanly as the simple theory predicts.

Check yourself
If the money supply M rises 8%, velocity V is unchanged, and real output Y is unchanged, what does the quantity equation imply about the price level P?
Correct — with V and Y both fixed, M×V=P×Y implies P must rise proportionally with M to keep the identity balanced, roughly 8%.

05

The Demand for Money

Why hold cash at all, instead of putting every last dollar into interest-bearing bonds? Three distinct reasons.

Three motives for holding money

Transactions-related: money held to pay for everyday goods and services. Grows with the overall scale of the economy — as GDP rises, so does the money needed to conduct all those transactions.

Precautionary: a buffer against unforeseen needs. Scales with the volume and value of the transactions a person or firm typically handles — a company doing millions in transactions needs a much bigger cash buffer than an individual.

Speculative: money held specifically because other assets (like bonds) look risky or unattractive right now. This is the motive most sensitive to interest rates.

The trade-off behind speculative demand

Holding money instead of bonds means giving up the return the bond would have paid — that foregone return is the opportunity cost of holding cash. Speculative money demand falls as returns on other assets rise (holding cash gets more expensive) and rises as the perceived risk of those other assets increases (cash starts looking safer by comparison).

Check yourself
Interest rates on bonds rise sharply. What happens to the speculative demand for money, all else equal?
Correct — as bond returns rise, the opportunity cost of holding non-interest-bearing cash rises too, reducing speculative money demand.

Notice a pattern: transactions and precautionary demand both scale with the size of the economy (GDP), while speculative demand is the one genuinely sensitive to interest rates — which is exactly the channel monetary policy leans on.

06

The Supply & Demand for Money — Equilibrium

The interest rate is, quite literally, the price of money.

Just like any other market, the money market has a supply curve and a demand curve, and they settle at an equilibrium price — except here, the "price" is the nominal interest rate.

The two curves

Money supply (MS) — vertical: at any given moment, there's a fixed nominal quantity of money in the economy, regardless of the interest rate.

Money demand (MD) — downward sloping: as interest rates rise, the opportunity cost of holding cash rises, so speculative money demand falls.

At interest rate I₀, money supply exactly matches money demand. Above I₀ (at I₁), there's an excess supply of money — people hold more cash than they want, so they buy bonds with it, pushing bond prices up and interest rates back down. Below I₀ (at I₂), there's excess demand for money — people sell bonds to raise cash, pushing bond prices down and interest rates back up. Either way, the market is pulled back to I₀.

Check yourself
The nominal interest rate sits above the money market's equilibrium rate. What is happening in the money market?
Correct — above equilibrium, money supply exceeds money demand; people use the excess cash to buy bonds, and rising bond prices push interest rates back down toward equilibrium.

This is exactly the lever central banks pull: by expanding the money supply (shifting MS to the right), a central bank pushes the equilibrium interest rate down — the standard mechanism behind an interest-rate cut.

07

The Fisher Effect

The interest rate you're quoted is never just about time — it's also a forecast of inflation baked directly into the number.

The Fisher effect
Rnom = Rreal + πe

Named for economist Irving Fisher: the nominal interest rate equals the required real rate of interest plus expected inflation.

The Fisher effect rests on money neutrality — the idea that, over the long run, the money supply affects nominal prices but not real economic variables. If that holds, the real interest rate stays stable over time, and it's expected inflation that does virtually all the moving whenever nominal rates change.

Worked example

Investors require a 2.5% real return. Expected inflation is 3.2% over the coming year.

Rnom = 2.5% + 3.2% = 5.7% — the nominal rate a lender should demand to be properly compensated for both giving up the use of their money and for the inflation eroding its future purchasing power.

A third piece — the risk premium

In practice, real-world nominal rates also carry a risk premium on top of the Fisher effect's two components, compensating lenders for genuine uncertainty about the future — inflation surprises, default risk, and so on. The full picture: nominal rate = real rate + expected inflation + risk premium.

Check yourself
The nominal interest rate on a bond is 6%, and expected inflation is 4%. According to the Fisher effect, what is the approximate real rate of interest?
Correct — rearranging Rnom = Rreal + πe: Rreal = 6% − 4% = 2%.

08

The Costs of Inflation

If every price in the economy rose by exactly the same amount, in perfect sync, would anyone actually care? The real damage comes from somewhere else entirely.

The thought experiment

Imagine every price — including wages — is perfectly indexed to inflation, and inflation is perfectly predictable at 10%. In that world, nobody would actually care: salaries rise 10%, prices rise 10%, everyone's real position is unchanged. The genuine cost of inflation comes specifically from the parts of this fantasy that don't hold in reality: prices aren't perfectly indexed, and inflation isn't perfectly predictable.

Expected vs. unexpected inflation

Expected inflation can be built into wage negotiations and contracts in advance — its costs are real but manageable.

Unexpected inflation is the genuinely costly one: when actual inflation surprises to the upside, borrowers benefit at lenders' expense (the real value of debt shrinks); when it surprises to the downside, lenders benefit at borrowers' expense (the real value of debt grows). Either way, wealth gets redistributed in ways nobody actually agreed to.

Worked example — the confused television manufacturer

A TV manufacturer sees TV prices rise 10% and assumes demand for televisions specifically has jumped. It expands the factory, hires more workers, and ramps up production — only to discover the 10% rise was actually a generalized, economy-wide price increase, not extra demand for TVs at all.

Now stuck with surplus inventory, excess factory capacity, and too many workers, it has to cut production and lay people off. This is the real cost of unanticipated inflation: it corrupts the informational content of prices, causing genuinely wasteful decisions that wouldn't have happened under stable, predictable prices.

Check yourself
Inflation comes in higher than almost everyone expected this year. Who benefits, and who is hurt, among borrowers and lenders?
Correct — when inflation surprises to the upside, the real value of a fixed nominal debt shrinks, benefiting the borrower at the lender's expense.

Why controlling inflation became a central policy goal: the 1970s and 1980s gave developed economies a real-world demonstration of high, unpredictable inflation's costs, which is exactly why price stability became — and remains — the overarching mandate of most modern central banks.
